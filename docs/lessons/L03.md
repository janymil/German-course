# L03 — Ich bin Jana — sein konjugieren

## Pedagogical Goal
Introduce the first fully conjugated verb — sein (to be). This is the most-used verb in German and unlocks a huge range of communicative acts: stating identity, nationality, profession, age, and feelings. The sie/Sie/sie ambiguity is addressed head-on because it is the #1 source of confusion for Slovak learners at this stage. German personal pronouns are mapped onto their Slovak equivalents.

## Communicative Goal (what learner can do after)
Po tejto lekcii viem povedať, kto som, koľko mám rokov, čím som a ako sa cítim — a viem sa opýtať na to isté iných ľudí.

## Grammar Focus
**sein** (byť) — present tense conjugation:
| Person | German | Slovak |
|--------|--------|--------|
| ich | bin | som |
| du | bist | si |
| er/sie/es | ist | je |
| wir | sind | sme |
| ihr | seid | ste |
| sie/Sie | sind | sú / ste (formálne) |

Critical issue: **sie** (she) = **ist**, but **sie** (they) = **sind**, and **Sie** (formal You) = **sind**. All three are spelled identically or near-identically; only context and capitalization of Sie disambiguates.

Profession rule: **NO article with professions after sein**: "Ich bin ~~eine~~ Studentin." This contrasts with English ("I am A student") and is counterintuitive.

## Vocabulary Field
Identity descriptors: professions, adjectives describing self, age expression.

## Slovak Contrastive Notes
- Slovak "byť" is also highly irregular — learners already have experience with an irregular "to be." Map directly: som→bin, si→bist, je→ist, sme→sind, ste→seid, sú→sind.
- Slovak also omits the subject pronoun in conversation (it's encoded in the verb), but German REQUIRES IT. "Bin 28 Jahre alt" is wrong — must say "Ich bin 28 Jahre alt."
- Professions in Slovak also often drop the article in predicative position, so this rule is familiar.
- Slovak uses "mám X rokov" (I have X years) for age. German uses "ich bin X Jahre alt" (I am X years old). Common error: *"Ich habe 28 Jahre alt"* — WRONG!
- The formal Sie looks like third-person plural sie — the capital S is the only distinguisher in writing, but in speech there is none. Context is everything. This is genuinely confusing and should be acknowledged honestly.

## Exercise Sequence and Rationale
1. **Flashcard** — Paradigm cards: show pronoun → flip to correct sein form. Builds the full paradigm.
2. **MCQ** — Situational: multi-choice "which form is correct?" + one question specifically on sie/Sie/sie.
3. **Fill** — Complete the sentence with correct sein form: "Jana ___ 28 Jahre alt."
4. **Listen** — Hear a sentence → identify whether the speaker is talking about herself, someone else, or speaking formally. Trains context-deduction.
5. **Match** — Pronoun ↔ sein form. Consolidates the paradigm.

## Jana's Narrative Context
Jana píše profil na LinkedIn po nemecky. Potrebuje napísať: meno, vek, povolanie. "Ich bin Jana. Ich bin 28 Jahre alt. Ich bin Marketingmanagerin." Tiež si precvičuje, ako sa opýtať nového kolegu: "Wie alt bist du?" a šéfa: "Wie alt sind Sie?" — formálne a neformálne, to je dôležité.

## Teacher Notes
- The sie/Sie/sie triangle is genuinely the most confusing thing in early German for most learners. Don't skip it — address it directly in this lesson with a visual chart.
- Emphasize: in speech, only context (are you talking to your boss or your friend?) distinguishes Sie (formal) from sie (they). Native speakers navigate this automatically; learners need to build the habit consciously.
- The "no article with professions" rule should be drilled with contrast: "Das ist eine Lehrerin" (subject description, article OK) vs "Sie ist Lehrerin" (predicate, no article). This distinction comes again in L07.
- Slovak learners may over-use "mám X rokov" mentally and produce "Ich habe 28 Jahre." This L03/L05 pair must cross-reference to prevent this.
- Age question: "Wie alt bist du?" — answer: "Ich bin 28 Jahre alt." NOT "Ich bin 28 Jahr alt" (Jahre is plural).
