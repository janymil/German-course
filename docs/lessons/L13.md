## Grammar Notes

### Possessive Pronoun Endings

Possessive pronouns follow the **same endings as ein/eine/ein**:

| Person | Nominative M | Nominative F | Nominative N | Accusative M |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| ich | **mein** Vater | **meine** Mutter | **mein** Kind | **meinen** Vater |
| du | **dein** Bruder | **deine** Schwester | **dein** Bett | **deinen** Bruder |
| er | **sein** Name | **seine** Frau | **sein** Auto | **seinen** Namen |
| sie (sg) | **ihr** Mann | **ihre** Katze | **ihr** Haus | **ihren** Mann |
| wir | **unser** Vater | **unsere** Mutter | **unser** Kind | **unseren** Vater |
| ihr | **euer** Vater | **eure** Mutter | **euer** Kind | **euren** Vater |
| Sie/sie | **Ihr/ihr** Freund | **Ihre/ihre** Frau | **Ihr/ihr** Auto | **Ihren/ihren** Freund |

**Key trap**: `ihr` means FOUR different things:
1. ihr = her possessive (ihr Bruder = her brother)
2. ihr = your plural informal (euer is cleaner, but ihr is used)
3. Ihr = your formal (capital I!)
4. ihr = dative of sie (to her) — comes later

**euer** loses its -e- when it takes an ending: eure (not euere), euren (not eueren)!

## Teacher Notes
- The ihr/ihr/Ihr ambiguity is the single most confusing point. Address it head-on with context.
- euer → eure spelling change needs explicit drilling.
- the dialogue scene (video call to mom in Bratislava) is emotionally resonant — Jana misses home. Use this for motivation.
- Slovak has possessives too (môj, tvoj, jeho, jej, náš, váš) — the structure is familiar, endings are the new information.
