# L08 — Ein/eine/ein — Unbestimmter Artikel

## Pedagogical Goal
Complement L07 by introducing the indefinite article and — critically — establishing when to use ein/eine versus der/die/das. The first-mention / second-mention rule is the communicative key. This lesson also expands Jana's world beyond the apartment into Vienna's coffee-house culture, introducing the Kaffeehaus as a cultural anchor point. Learners gain the ability to describe unfamiliar objects and then refer back to them — a core skill in natural conversation.

## Communicative Goal (what learner can do after)
Po tejto lekcii viem správne používať ein/eine/ein pri prvej zmienke a der/die/das pri druhej zmienke, a opisovať veci v kaviarni.

## Grammar Focus
**Indefinite article** (neučitý člen):
| Gender | Definite | Indefinite |
|--------|---------|------------|
| M (der) | der | **ein** |
| F (die) | die | **eine** |
| N (das) | das | **ein** |

**First mention vs. second mention rule:**
- 1st mention (new, unknown): **ein/eine/ein**
  "Das ist **eine** Katze." (I'm introducing the cat for the first time)
- 2nd mention (known, established): **der/die/das**
  "**Die** Katze ist schwarz." (We both know which cat now)

**Zero article rules (preview):**
- After "sein" with professions: "Ich bin Kellnerin." (no article)
- Proper nouns (names, cities): Wien, Jana (no article)
- These are already known from L03 — now explained with the article system in mind.

## Vocabulary Field

## Slovak Contrastive Notes
- Slovak has NO ARTICLES at all. Neither definite nor indefinite. This means the entire article system is new cognitive territory — Slovak learners cannot rely on L1 transfer.
- The closest Slovak equivalent is the demonstrative pronoun: "tá mačka je čierna" (that cat is black) — emphasize: German uses der/die/das where Slovak would use ten/tá/to OR nothing.
- The first/second mention rule maps to a pragmatic principle Slovak speakers use intuitively (given vs. new information) — even if Slovak doesn't mark it with articles. Connect to this.
- Slovak "jedna" (one/a) is used as an indefinite marker in colloquial speech: "Tam sedí jedna žena" — parallel to German "eine Frau." This connection helps.
- The neutralization of gender in ein (M) vs ein (N) — both masculine and neuter have "ein" — is initially confusing but learners accept it once they see the pattern.

## Exercise Sequence and Rationale
1. **Flashcard** — New café nouns with their indefinite articles. Learner sees image+word, flips to see gender + article.
2. **MCQ** — 6 questions: choose ein/eine/ein or der/die/das based on context (first vs. second mention). Includes one question on zero article with professions.
3. **Fill** — Complete a café dialogue: "Das ist ___ Kaffee. ___ Kaffee ist sehr gut."
4. **Listen** — Hear a noun → say whether it's first mention (ein/eine) or second mention (der/die/das) based on context.
5. **Match** — Noun ↔ its indefinite article form.

## Teacher Notes
- The absence of articles in Slovak is the foundational challenge. Don't minimize it — acknowledge it explicitly: "Slovenčina nemá členy. To je náročné, pretože nemecká gramatika závisí od členov."
- The first/second mention rule is the most communicatively useful way to present articles. Grammar books sometimes lead with the formal/indefinite distinction — but the functional approach (new vs. known) is more memorable.
- Note in the Jana narrative: "Ich nehme einen Kaffee" is a preview of the accusative (L10). If learners notice it, acknowledge it: "Dobre si si všimol! Naučíme sa prečo v lekcii 10."
- Vienna coffee culture is rich and authentic context — Melange, Verlängerter, Einspänner are Austrian specialties. Flag them as cultural notes without requiring learners to memorize.
- Café scenario recycling: all previous grammar (sein, haben, W-questions, regular verbs, articles) can be embedded naturally in a café conversation.
