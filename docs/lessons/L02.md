# L02 — Hallo! Grüßen und Vorstellen

## Pedagogical Goal
Deliver the learner's first functional communicative payoff: the ability to greet, farewell, and introduce themselves by name. This lesson is kept high-frequency and low-grammar-load intentionally — the goal is early success and motivation. Every phrase taught here will recur in every subsequent lesson as lesson scaffolding. Austrian variants (Grüß Gott, Servus) are flagged immediately because Jana is moving to Vienna, not Germany.

## Communicative Goal (what learner can do after)
Po tejto lekcii viem pozdraviť kolegu alebo šéfa, rozlúčiť sa a predstaviť sa menom — formálne aj neformálne.

## Grammar Focus
No explicit grammar — but the formal/informal distinction (Sie vs. du) is introduced functionally, without full conjugation (that comes in L03). The split is felt, not yet analyzed. "Wie heißen Sie?" vs. "Wie heißt du?" is presented as a memorized chunk.

## Vocabulary Field
Social ritual language: greetings, farewells, politeness markers, self-introduction formulas.

## Slovak Contrastive Notes
- Slovak has a similar formal/informal split (Vy/ty) — learners understand the concept immediately.
- "Sie" (formal You) looks like "ona/oni" in German — this will be addressed fully in L03. For now, treat it as a formula.
- German greetings are time-of-day-specific (Morgen/Tag/Abend) — Slovak uses "Dobrý deň" more broadly. German speakers appreciate temporal precision.
- "Bitte" is multipurpose: "Prosím" (please) + "Nech sa páči" (here you go / you're welcome). Slovak learners need both meanings.
- "Entschuldigung" = "Prepáčte" — it covers both "Excuse me" (getting attention) and a light apology.
- "Es tut mir leid" = "Je mi ľúto" — reserved for more genuine apologies. Learners often confuse the two.
- Austrian "Grüß Gott" is ubiquitous in Vienna — more common than "Guten Tag" in shops, offices. Jana MUST know this on day one in Vienna.
- "Servus" is informal Austrian/Bavarian — same word for hello AND goodbye (like Slovak "Čau").

## Exercise Sequence and Rationale
1. **Flashcard** — All 14 active phrases: German → Slovak. First exposure requires low-stakes retrieval.
2. **MCQ** — Situational: "You meet your new boss at 9 AM. What do you say?" Tests contextual appropriateness over mere translation.
3. **Fill** — Complete the greeting/farewell in context. Forces production, not just recognition.
4. **Listen** — Hear the phrase → identify it. Trains ear for natural speed German greetings.
5. **Match** — German phrase ↔ Slovak meaning OR formal ↔ informal pair.

## Jana's Narrative Context
Jana dostala e-mail z viedenskej firmy: príde na pohovor o týždeň. Cvičí si základné frázy. Pred zrkadlom opakuje: "Guten Tag, mein Name ist Jana Nováková." Vie, že vo Viedni hovoria "Grüß Gott" — naučila sa to od rakúskej spolužiačky.

## Teacher Notes
- Frame every phrase within a real Vienna scenario (entering a shop, meeting a colleague, answering the phone). Abstract memorization fails adult learners.
- The formal/informal distinction is intuitive for Slovak speakers — use this explicitly: "Je to rovnaké ako Vy/ty po slovensky."
- "Grüß Gott" should be drilled MORE than "Guten Tag" for this learner profile (Vienna-bound).
- "Bitte" as a response to "Danke" will confuse learners used to Slovak "prosím" only meaning "please." Give both examples immediately.
- Don't overwhelm with Austrian variants — two (Grüß Gott, Servus) are sufficient at A1. Heuristic: what will Jana hear on her first day at work?
- The lesson intentionally has NO grammar analysis. Cognitive load is low — let learners enjoy the communicative win.
