# L06 — Zahlen 21–100 und Preise

## Pedagogical Goal
Complete the number system to 100 and introduce regular -en verb conjugation — the largest category of German verbs. By tackling both in one lesson, learners gain enormous productive capacity: they can now describe prices, quantities, and perform a wide range of actions. The "units before tens" pattern (einundzwanzig) is counterintuitive for Slovak speakers and is the core motor-memory challenge of this lesson. The irregular dreißig spelling is flagged.

## Communicative Goal (what learner can do after)
Po tejto lekcii viem počítať do 100, povedať ceny v eurách, opýtať sa koľko niečo stojí a časovať pravidelné slovesá.

## Grammar Focus
**Numbers 21–100:**
- Pattern: UNITS + "und" + TENS: einundzwanzig (21), zweiunddreißig (32), etc.
- dreißig (30) — irregular spelling (NOT dreissig! The -ß- persists here — special case)
- Round tens: zwanzig(20), dreißig(30), vierzig(40), fünfzig(50), sechzig(60), siebzig(70), achtzig(80), neunzig(90), hundert(100)
- Note: sechzig drops the final -s of sechs (like sechzehn); siebzig drops -en of sieben (like siebzehn)

**Regular -en verb conjugation:**
| Person | stem + ending | kaufen example |
|--------|--------------|----------------|
| ich | -e | kaufe |
| du | -st | kaufst |
| er/sie/es | -t | kauft |
| wir | -en | kaufen |
| ihr | -t | kauft |
| sie/Sie | -en | kaufen |

**Special case: stems ending in -t or -d** (arbeiten, finden):
- e is inserted before -st/-t endings for pronounceability:
- du arbeit**e**st, er arbeit**e**t (NOT arbeitst/arbeitt)

**Prices:** "Das kostet drei Euro fünfzig." / "Wie viel kostet das?"

## Vocabulary Field
Numbers 21–100, prices/shopping, everyday verbs (kaufen, kosten, wohnen, lernen, arbeiten).

## Slovak Contrastive Notes
- Slovak uses TENS before UNITS: "dvadsaťjeden" (twenty-one). German inverts: Ein-und-zwanzig (one-and-twenty). This is the primary challenge — learners will habitually think "dvadsaťjeden" and say "zwanzigein" (wrong). Intensive drilling required.
- dreißig: Slovak "tridsať" has no orthographic irregularity. German learners must memorize this one exception.
- Regular verb conjugation in Slovak is more complex (7 conjugation classes). German's one dominant pattern (-e/-st/-t/-en/-t/-en) will feel elegantly simple to Slovak learners.
- The -e insertion in arbeiten (arbeitest, arbeitet) mirrors a similar euphonic process in Slovak (e.g., brať → beriem), so learners understand the principle even if the specifics differ.
- "Euro fünfzig" for prices — Slovak says "euro päťdesiat." The omission of "Cent" in German colloquial prices is parallel to Slovak practice.

## Exercise Sequence and Rationale
1. **Flashcard** — Round numbers 20–100 + key two-digit examples (21, 35, 47, 63, 99).
2. **MCQ** — 6 questions: number writing (unit-before-ten trap), dreißig spelling, verb conjugation (kaufen, arbeiten), price expression.
3. **Fill** — Write numbers in words + complete verb conjugation table + price sentences.
4. **Listen** — Hear a price → write digits. (Jana is price-checking items for her new apartment.)
5. **Match** — Number digit ↔ German word.

## Jana's Narrative Context
Jana je v IKEA vo Viedni. Kupuje veci pre nový byt. Pozerá sa na cenovky: "Das kostet neununddreißig Euro neunzig." Pýta sa predavačky: "Wie viel kostet das Regal?" Predavačka odpovedá: "Achtundsechzig Euro fünfzig." Jana počíta, či má dosť peňazí.

## Teacher Notes
- The unit-before-ten pattern is a serious stumbling block. Use a memorable mnemonic: "In German, the little number comes first — like a child who pushes ahead!" Or reference the nursery rhyme counting pattern.
- dreißig: emphasize it every time — it LOOKS wrong but is correct. Slovak learners will want to write "dreissig."
- Verbs: the regular -en pattern is the "default" German verb blueprint. Once learners internalize it, all future verbs are easy to conjugate unless marked otherwise.
- arbeiten as an example of -e insertion: don't call it "irregular" — it's a regular phonological rule. The term "irregular" should be reserved for truly irregular verbs (sein, haben, modal verbs).
- Shopping scenario is ideal — Jana needs items for her new apartment throughout weeks 1–4.
