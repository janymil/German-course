# L09 — Woher kommst du? — Länder und Sprachen

## Pedagogical Goal
Expand Jana's self-description toolkit to include nationality, origin, and languages. This lesson introduces a critical grammar exception: countries with definite articles (die Slowakei, die Schweiz, die Türkei, die USA) require a dative-governed article after "aus" (aus der Slowakei, aus der Schweiz). This is a preview of the dative case — presented functionally, not analytically. Negation with "nicht" is formally introduced here (previously only kein was previewed in L05).

## Communicative Goal (what learner can do after)
Po tejto lekcii viem povedať odkiaľ pochádzam, kde bývam, aké jazyky hovorím — a to isté sa opýtať iných ľudí.

## Grammar Focus
**Countries with NO article** (most countries):
- Ich komme aus Deutschland. / aus Österreich. / aus England. / aus Tschechien.
- Ich wohne in Frankreich. / in Italien.

**Countries WITH definite article "die"** (MEMORIZE this list):
- die Slowakei → aus **der** Slowakei (aus + dative: die → der)
- die Schweiz → aus **der** Schweiz
- die Türkei → aus **der** Türkei
- die USA → aus **den** USA (plural, dative plural → den)
- Note: "aus der" — the feminines use der in dative (fully treated in L12)

**kommen aus** + country: origin
**wohnen in** + city/country: current residence
**sprechen** — irregular in 2nd/3rd person: du sprichst, er/sie/es spricht (stem vowel change e→i)

**Negation with nicht:**
- Negates verbs/adjectives/adverbs: "Ich spreche **nicht** Englisch." (I don't speak English)
- Position: after the verb (usually), before what is being negated
- Contrast with kein (L05 preview): nicht negates non-noun; kein negates nouns with articles

**Languages as adjectives from country names:** Deutschland → Deutsch; Österreich → [use Deutsch]; Slowakei → Slowakisch; England → Englisch. Languages are always capitalized in German.

## Vocabulary Field
Countries, nationalities, languages, "aus" + dative preview.

## Slovak Contrastive Notes
- Slovak also uses "z + genitive" for origin: "Som z Bratislavy." The concept maps — but German uses dative (not genitive) after "aus." This will be fully clear in L12; for now, just memorize "aus der Slowakei" as a chunk.
- "Wohnen in" is parallel to Slovak "bývam v" — direct mapping.
- Capitalization of languages: In Slovak, jazyky are lowercase ("po slovensky"). In German, they're UPPERCASE nouns: "Ich spreche **Deutsch**." Flag this explicitly.
- The sprechen stem-vowel change (e→i) also occurs in Slovak irregular verbs (brať→beriem), so learners conceptually accept it. Map it: "Volá sa to podobne ako v slovakistike — striedanie samohlásky."
- nicht vs. kein: Slovak uses "nie" for both. German distinguishes. This is a major point of divergence.

## Teacher Notes
- The article-country group is small but VERY commonly needed. Memorize as a LIST, not a rule (because there's no reliable rule for which countries get "die").
- "aus der Slowakei" — the dative form "der" for feminine nouns is a preview of the dative case. Don't explain dative fully here. Just say: "Po 'aus' sa 'die' mení na 'der' — naučíme sa prečo v lekcii 12."
- sprechen e→i: this type of strong verb vowel change recurs throughout German. Pattern: ich spreche (e), du sprichst (i), er spricht (i), wir sprechen (e). Flag as "strong verb" phenomenon without overwhelming.
- The nicht/kein contrast: nicht negates the predicate or elements other than indefinite noun phrases; kein negates nouns with indefinite articles or no article. "Ich habe keine Zeit" (kein) vs. "Das stimmt nicht" (nicht). Simple rule to start: use kein with nouns, nicht with everything else.
