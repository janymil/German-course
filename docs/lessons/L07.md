# L07 — Artikel der/die/das — Nominativ

## Pedagogical Goal
Introduce grammatical gender — the most foreign concept for Slovak learners (Slovak has gender but its assignment often follows different patterns). The key pedagogical intervention in this lesson is: teach learners to ALWAYS learn nouns with their article, never bare. The mnemonic "der/die/das ist Teil des Wortes" (the article is part of the word) is established as an axiom. Gender-signal suffixes are introduced as productive rules, not memory burdens. The nominative case is introduced as a concept (subject = wer/was) in preparation for accusative in L10.

## Communicative Goal (what learner can do after)
Po tejto lekcii viem správne pomenovať 15 každodenných predmetov s ich rodom a použiť ich v jednoduchých vetách.

## Grammar Focus
**Grammatical gender** — three genders in German:
| Gender | Definite article | Indefinite article | Slovak equivalent |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Maskulinum | **der** | ein | ten/jeden |
| Femininum | **die** | eine | tá/jedna |
| Neutrum | **das** | ein | to/jedno |

**Nominative case** = the subject of the sentence (wer? was? — kto? čo?):
- "**Der Tisch** ist groß." → Der Tisch is the subject (wer/was ist groß?)
- Nominative form = "dictionary form" — same as what you look up in a dictionary.

**Gender signals (suffix clues):**
- → **die**: -ung (Wohnung), -heit (Gesundheit), -keit (Möglichkeit), -schaft (Freundschaft), -ion (Station), -ie (Energie)
- → **der**: -er (Lehrer), -ig (König), -ling (Lehrling), -ismus (Tourismus)
- → **das**: infinitives used as nouns (das Lernen), -chen (Mädchen), -lein (Fräulein), -ment (Moment)
- Beware: these are TENDENCIES, not absolute rules. Exceptions exist.

**The golden rule:** IMMER den Artikel mitlernen! (Always learn the article with the noun!)

## Vocabulary Field
Everyday household/apartment items — Jana's new Vienna apartment.

## Slovak Contrastive Notes
- Slovak has 3 genders too (mužský/ženský/stredný), but German gender assignment often differs. Do NOT assume "stôl" (M in Slovak) = "der Tisch" — always verify. (Actually Tisch IS der, but many don't match.)
- Slovak marks gender in adjective endings (malý/malá/malé) — German does too, but the system differs.
- Slovak learners have the cognitive framework for gender, which is a major advantage. Unlike English speakers who must learn the concept from scratch, Slovak learners just need to learn WHICH German nouns have WHICH gender.
- The article system in German encodes more information than Slovak demonstrative pronouns — it will carry case information too (Lesson 10+).
- Slovak uses "ten/tá/to" as demonstratives; German der/die/das serve as both article and demonstrative. "Der ist groß" = "Ten je veľký." — same word, two functions.

## Exercise Sequence and Rationale
1. **Flashcard** — Noun → article + Slovak translation. Force retrieval of the article, not just the word.
2. **MCQ** — 6 questions on gender assignment: Is it der/die/das? Include suffix-clue questions.
3. **Fill** — Insert correct definite article in context sentences about Jana's apartment.
4. **Listen** — Hear a noun (without article) → choose der/die/das. Trains immediate gender recall.
5. **Match** — Noun ↔ article. 15 pairs (all lesson vocab).

## Teacher Notes
- The "always learn with the article" rule is SO important that it bears repeating in every single subsequent lesson. Make it a mantra.
- Suffix signals are genuinely useful and productive. Present them as "detective clues," not grammar rules — learners should enjoy applying them to new words.
- Avoid drilling exceptions immediately — first build confidence with the rules, exceptions can come as enrichment later.
- The nominative case is introduced as a concept here but not stressed — the focus is on articles and gender. Nominative vs. accusative contrast comes in L10.
- Use Jana's apartment as a concrete memory anchor — every noun is a physical object she has. This episodic encoding dramatically boosts retention.
- Mnemonic suggestion: color-code by gender (der=blue, die=red, das=green) across all lesson materials. Research supports this for German gender.
