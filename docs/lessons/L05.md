# L05 — Zahlen 0–20 und haben

## Pedagogical Goal
Introduce numbers 0–20 and the second critical verb: haben (to have). Numbers unlock age, phone numbers, prices (previewed here, expanded in L06), and quantity expressions. The haben/sein contrast is introduced: Slovak learners with English background may try to say "Ich habe 28 Jahre" (calque from "I'm 28 years old" perceived as "I have 28 years") — this error must be corrected pre-emptively. Two irregular number forms (sechzehn, siebzehn) are explicitly flagged.

## Communicative Goal (what learner can do after)
Po tejto lekcii viem povedať čísla 0–20, povedať koľko mám rokov, dať niekomu svoje telefónne číslo a povedať, čo mám (rodinu, veci).

## Grammar Focus
**haben** (mať) — present tense:
| Person | haben | byť (contrast) |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| ich | habe | bin |
| du | hast | bist |
| er/sie/es | hat | ist |
| wir | haben | sind |
| ihr | habt | seid |
| sie/Sie | haben | sind |

**Numbers 0–20** with two irregular forms:
- sechzehn (16): NOT sechszehn (the final S of sechs drops before -zehn)
- siebzehn (17): NOT siebenzehn (the -en of sieben drops before -zehn)

**haben vs sein for age:**
- CORRECT: "Ich **bin** 28 Jahre alt." (sein + age)
- WRONG: ~~"Ich habe 28 Jahre."~~ (German does NOT express age with haben)

**kein/keine** preview: Ich habe **keine** Schwester. (negation of nouns with haben — full treatment in L10)

## Vocabulary Field
Numbers 0–20, family members, phone vocabulary, basic possession expressions.

## Slovak Contrastive Notes
- Slovak uses "mám X rokov" (I have X years) — the translation suggests haben, but German uses sein. This is the #1 haben/sein confusion error. Flag prominently.
- Slovak numbers 1–20 are irregular in similar ways (pätnásť, šestnásť) — the concept of irregular teen numbers is familiar. Focus learners on the TWO German irregulars.
- "null" — Slovak uses "nula" for math but "nula" or individual digits for phone numbers. German uses "null" throughout.
- "Telefonnummer" — compound noun, characteristic of German. Show how German builds compound nouns (Telefon + Nummer) without spaces or hyphens. This is introduced as a feature, fully treated later.

## Exercise Sequence and Rationale
1. **Flashcard** — Numbers 0–20 with Slovak translations. Two cards specially marked for sechzehn/siebzehn irregulars.
2. **MCQ** — 5 questions: 2 on number spelling (sechzehn/siebzehn traps), 2 on haben conjugation, 1 on haben vs sein for age.
3. **Fill** — Write out numbers in words + complete haben sentences.
4. **Listen** — Hear a number → write it in digits. Simulates phone number dictation.
5. **Match** — Number word ↔ digit.

## Jana's Narrative Context
Jana vypĺňa formulár pre novú prácu. Recepčná sa jej pýta: "Wie ist Ihre Telefonnummer?" Jana zarecituje: "null-sieben-eins-vier-..." Tiež musí vyplniť kolónku "Alter" — napíše "28" a vie, že po nemecky povie "Ich bin achtundzwanzig Jahre alt" (čísla nad 20 naučí zajtra!).

## Teacher Notes
- Numbers 0–20 must be learned to automaticity — they appear in prices (L06), years, ages, and phone numbers constantly throughout the curriculum.
- The sechzehn/siebzehn irregulars are persistent error sources. Use red/warning formatting in the lesson UI.
- haben paradigm: the er/sie/es form "hat" is the only truly irregular form (others follow -en verb pattern). Contrast with sein to show that haben is "less irregular."
- kein/keine preview in "Ich habe keine Schwester" — don't over-explain. Just note "kein = kein/žiadny." Full grammar comes in L10.
- Phone number convention: Germans say digits individually, or in pairs. "null eins sieben..." not "null-einsieben." Practice dictation.
